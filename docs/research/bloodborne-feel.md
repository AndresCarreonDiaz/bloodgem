# Research: bloodborne-feel

## Summary

Bloodborne's feel comes from one interlocking design thesis: aggression is survival. The Rally/Regain system gives you roughly a 5-second window after taking damage to win lost health back by attacking, and as Inverse's 10-year retrospective put it, "this single change completely alters the way you approach a Souls experience." Everything else reinforces it: shields are gone (replaced by a gun used as a parry tool that opens enemies to invulnerable, high-damage visceral attacks), the quickstep dash is faster than a Dark Souls roll (11 i-frames, 18-frame recovery vs DS3's 13 i-frames / 21-frame recovery — faster but less forgiving), backsteps have zero i-frames so players learn to dodge INTO attacks, and trick weapons reward mid-combo transformation attacks that deal more damage for less stamina than a normal heavy. Reviewers consistently describe the result as combat that feels "like a dance" of traded strikes rather than Dark Souls' siege of attrition.

Atmospherically, the game is structured as a controlled escalation from gothic Victorian dread to cosmic horror — critics almost universally describe it as "Gothic Horror for the first half and cosmic horror for the second." This is delivered mechanically, not just narratively: the hunt night advances on boss milestones (dusk, then night after Vicar Amelia, then the Blood Moon after Rom), the Insight stat literally re-renders the world at numeric thresholds (enemies gain new attacks at 15+, invisible Amygdalas appear on buildings at 40+, the Hunter's Dream music changes at 50, a crying baby follows you at 60), and the score shifts from acoustic/orchestral to processed electronic timbres as the horror turns cosmic, with music largely reserved for bosses so exploration stays in oppressive ambient sound. Yharnam itself is the other half of the atmosphere: vertically stacked (rooftops/streets/sewers) architecture that "bears down on you," with Central Yharnam's famous loop of one-way shortcuts (gates, ladders, drops) that repeatedly spits players back at the first lamp — "an open area that interconnects with itself like any city in the real world" (TheGamer).

The emotional arc players describe — oppression, then mastery, then awe — is engineered by specific gates. Father Gascoigne is the aggression exam (forum lore holds that a large share of players never beat him; those who do report "extreme elation" and being "practically set for what the game can throw at you"), the post-Amelia and post-Rom world-state changes are the awe payoffs ("Behold! A Paleblood sky!"), and the one widely-agreed design failure is blood vial farming — consumable healing that forces grinding exactly when players are stuck on a boss, a mistake FromSoftware implicitly conceded by returning to refilling flasks in Sekiro and Elden Ring. For Bloodgem, the actionable core is: build the rally loop, tune a fast trust-the-dash quickstep, make one tight interconnected first level, escalate the world-state on boss kills, and do not make healing farmable.

## Key findings

- Rally/Regain system (Fextralife wiki): after taking damage you have 5 seconds to recover lost health by landing attacks; heavy attacks regain more than light; visceral attacks restore the maximum possible; the Guidance Caryll rune boosts rally by 10%/20%; some bosses (e.g. Darkbeast Paarl) only offer regain on certain attacks.
- Dodge frame data (bloodborne-wiki.com): rolls and quicksteps both grant 11 i-frames and cost 10 stamina; forward dash has an 18-frame recovery ('roll cancel'), side/back 20 frames; Dark Souls 3 fast roll is 13 i-frames / 21-frame recovery — Bloodborne's dash is ~3 frames faster with fewer i-frames; the short backstep has 0 i-frames.
- Quickstep vs roll: locked-on + direction + circle = quickstep, unlocked = roll; the wiki notes 'there's no major difference between rolling and quickstepping' in protection — the difference is spacing and speed.
- Gun parry / visceral (Fextralife): shooting during an enemy's attack windup staggers them into a visceral-attack state; viscerals deal Thrust damage, scale with the Skill stat and Blood Level, and grant full invulnerability during the animation; ammo (Quicksilver Bullets) is limited, creating a risk-reward economy.
- Trick weapons (Saw Cleaver, Fextralife/Fandom wikis): untransformed mode = rapid, low-stamina serrated attacks with +20% damage vs beast enemies; transformed = slower, longer-range, higher damage; the mid-combo transformation attack 'does more damage than an R2, comes out faster, for a smaller stamina cost' — mastery means weaving transforms into combos, not menu-swapping.
- Inverse (Hayes Madsen, 10-year anniversary): 'This lets you recover portions of lost health by attacking the enemy, and this single change completely alters the way you approach a Souls experience'; 'The game's combat and character progression are designed to feed into that core idea of aggression'; on atmosphere: 'oppressive feeling of isolation... The city's twisted architecture bears down on you.'
- Mancunion retrospective: 'players are implicitly encouraged to dodge towards enemies'; combat becomes 'a rhythmic exchange rather than defensive positioning'; the aesthetic is 'the Latin choral soundtrack, the gothic architecture, the intermingling of the sacred and the profane.'
- Tone structure (Grimdark Magazine): 'the game is best considered to be Gothic Horror for the first half and cosmic horror for the second'; 'punishing for beginners but gradually you will learn the rhyme and reason of the combat system'; early enemies are deliberately mundane — 'human mobs, Wolfman-esque enemies, and full-on werewolves' — saving cosmic entities for act two.
- Sound design ('Hail the Nightmare' academic paper, Open University): the score shifts 'from acoustic and orchestral instruments to a more processed, electronic, and synth sound' as gothic gives way to cosmic horror; 'persistently discordant harmonies ensure the music never offers the player any sensation of victory'; players are conditioned to associate musical phenomena with otherworldly creatures and death — exploration is otherwise music-free ambient dread.
- Insight thresholds (Fextralife/TheGamer insight guides): 15+ Insight gives enemies new moves (Church Doctors fire projectiles from their lamps), 40+ makes Amygdalas visible clinging to Oedon Chapel and Yahar'gul's gates without the Blood Moon, 50 changes the Hunter's Dream music, 60 makes a baby's crying follow you everywhere; higher insight also lowers Frenzy resistance — knowledge is mechanically dangerous.
- Hunt night structure: world time advances on story milestones, not a clock — evening at start, night after Vicar Amelia, Blood Moon after Rom the Vacuous Spider — the same map degenerates around the player, with enemy populations and skybox swapped.
- Central Yharnam level design (TheGamer): 'Shortcuts, hidden areas, and later levels are visible from the get-go'; a gate 'loops back into Iosefka's Clinic'; a ladder gives 'a shortcut to the lamp that you'd otherwise have to cross the bridge to find'; a locked gate near the start only opens after the Forbidden Woods, revealing an expanded clinic area; 'You can get from A to B in various different ways, with each different path rewarding you with items, lore, or methods to make bosses easier'; the area 'interconnects with itself like any city in the real world.' Yharnam is layered vertically — rooftops, streets, and sewers stacked.
- GMTK's 'Redesigning Death' (Mark Brown, March 30 2015) was directly inspired by 'Bloodborne's clever twist on MMO corpse-running': lost blood echoes can be absorbed by a nearby enemy (marked by glowing eyes), turning recovery into a hunt instead of a corpse-tag.
- Blood vial criticism (NeoGAF threads, widely echoed): consumable healing that must be farmed 'adds a grind' precisely when players are stuck repeating a hard boss — 'making the experience worse for players who struggle'; defenders argue the rally system is the intended primary healing; FromSoftware reverted to auto-refilling healing in Sekiro and Elden Ring, widely read as conceding the flaw.
- Father Gascoigne as the aggression exam (ResetEra/Game Voyagers): forum consensus holds more than half of players never got past him; beating him produces 'extreme elation,' and 'once you are able to beat him, you're practically set for what the game can throw at you' — he punishes passive Dark Souls habits and forces internalizing rally + dodge-in + parry.
- Reception (Wikipedia): 2015 Game of the Year from Eurogamer, GameTrailers, Destructoid, and Edge; IGN's PlayStation 4 Game of the Year; critics' most-repeated feel descriptors are 'aggression,' 'adrenaline,' 'dance,' and 'oppressive atmosphere.'
- The awe payoff players cite is the post-Rom reveal — 'Behold! A Paleblood sky!' — when the Blood Moon recontextualizes everything seen so far; the Mancunion frames the theme as: 'in our attempts to prove our superior statuses as men, we prove ourselves to be just as (if not more) vicious as the same beasts we reject.'

## Actionable for Bloodgem

- Build the rally bar first — it IS the Bloodborne feeling. On taking damage, render the lost HP as an orange 'recoverable' segment that drains over ~4-5 seconds; each landed hit converts part of it back, scaled by attack weight (heavy > light, finisher = full). Make this the primary healing loop and tune enemy damage assuming players use it.
- Tune the quickstep to Bloodborne's exact ratios at 60fps: ~11 frames (~180ms) of i-frames inside an ~18-20 frame (~300-330ms) total dash, 10-stamina cost allowing 3-4 chained dashes. Give a locked-on short quickstep and a longer unlocked roll. Critically: give the backward hop 0 i-frames so the meta becomes dodging INTO and THROUGH attacks, not away.
- Gun parry as an interrupt, not a damage source: a small timing window aligned to enemy windup animations; success staggers into a 'visceral' state where a contextual finisher deals massive damage, grants full i-frames during the animation, and refunds the entire rally segment. Use scarce ammo, purchasable or convertible from your own HP (blood bullets), for risk-reward.
- Implement 2-3 trick weapons instead of many flat ones: each has two movesets (fast/short/cheap vs slow/long/expensive) plus a transform attack usable mid-combo that deals MORE damage than a heavy for LESS stamina — so fluent form-weaving is mechanically optimal, and mastery is visible in play. Add a serration-style +20% damage tag vs beast-class enemies on one weapon.
- Copy Central Yharnam's loop grammar for your first level: one lamp (checkpoint), a long dangerous outbound path, and 2-3 one-way shortcuts (gate unlocked from the far side, kickable ladder, elevator) that all funnel back to the lamp. In top-down 2.5D, do 'verticality' with stacked layers — bridges crossing over earlier streets, sewer sublayers, balconies — and show later areas early through fences, chasms, and lower layers visible from above.
- Steal the corpse-run twist GMTK highlighted: dropped blood echoes can be swallowed by a nearby enemy who gains glowing eyes and slightly buffed stats — recovery becomes a hunt, on-theme and cheap to implement.
- Add an Insight-style knowledge stat with hard numeric thresholds that mutate the world: threshold 1 gives some enemies new attacks, threshold 2 renders previously-invisible horrors in the background layer (trivial with conditional Canvas sprite layers), threshold 3 swaps ambient audio stems (whispers, a crying baby). Optionally trade it as a currency so spending knowledge is a real choice.
- Structure the night on boss milestones, not a clock: dusk -> night -> blood moon palette/sky swaps plus enemy-population swaps on the SAME maps after key bosses. This is Bloodborne's cheapest, highest-impact atmosphere trick — the world degenerates around the player and rewards backtracking through shortcuts.
- Audio rule: no music during exploration — only ambient dread (wind, distant howls, mob chants, footsteps on cobblestone) — and reserve discordant choral/string music for boss arenas. In the late cosmic-horror act, shift the instrument palette toward processed/synth textures. Never let the score resolve into triumphant harmony mid-game.
- Do NOT ship farmable consumable healing. Either use refilling charges at checkpoints (the Sekiro/Elden Ring correction) or, if you keep consumables for flavor, make rally strong enough that vials are a backstop and auto-top-up the stock from banked echoes on respawn so a stuck player never has to grind.
- Design your 'Gascoigne gate': an early mandatory boss (a fellow hunter-type with your own moveset, two phases) that is nearly unbeatable with passive play but collapses once the player uses rally, dodge-in, and parry. Follow it immediately with an awe payoff — a vista reveal and the first night-state change.
- Plan the emotional arc as content sequencing: act one is grounded gothic (mobs with torches, wolf-beasts, claustrophobic streets); hold all cosmic entities until a mid-game 'Rom moment' that flips the blood-moon state and makes hidden horrors visible everywhere, retroactively recontextualizing areas the player thought they knew. That oppression -> mastery -> awe pipeline is what players mean by 'the Bloodborne feeling.'
