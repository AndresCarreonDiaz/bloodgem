# Research: pixel-beauty

## Summary

The gap between "cheap" and "premium" pixel art environments is rarely the tiles themselves — it is what's layered on top of them. Hyper Light Drifter is flat color + huge soft gradient/vignette overlays + sunbeams; Eastward is hand-painted tiles + a lighting pass + blue-shadow LUT grading; CrossCode is 16px tiles + off-grid prop placement + multi-layer environment particles; Celeste hides its 8px grid with 1-4 random variants per edge tile and abstract infill. All of these are screen-space or sprite-layer tricks that map directly onto Canvas2D via offscreen canvases and globalCompositeOperation ('lighter' for lights, 'multiply' for ambient/grade). The consistent hierarchy of wins per effort: (1) a multiply-composited lightmap with additive light blobs, (2) per-area color grading tints, (3) ambient particles on 2-3 depth layers, (4) an off-grid decal/prop layer, (5) tile variants + transition tiles, (6) height-tier shadow/highlight/parallax cues, (7) crossfaded 2-frame water with sparkle overlay. The consistent "cheap" tells to avoid: mixed pixel densities, non-integer scaling/rotation of sprites, pillow shading, too many similar colors, heavy vignette/CRT, and bare repeating tiles with no overlay work.

## Key findings

- LIGHTING IS THE #1 SEPARATOR. Hyper Light Drifter's look is literally 'flat colour as the basis... with large gradients or vignettes overlayed on top' — smooth soft-light gradients composited over sharp pixels at partial opacity, which 'adds variety and fidelity without adding much extra work'. Eastward runs full deferred lighting over pixel art; Octopath/HD-2D is pixel sprites + dynamic light + tilt-shift diorama framing. Smooth (non-pixelated) light gradients over crisp pixel art are an accepted, premium-reading technique — you do NOT need to quantize the lighting.
- THE STANDARD 2D LIGHTMAP TECHNIQUE (slembcke.net) is Canvas2D-native: render the scene, fill an offscreen canvas with the ambient color, draw fuzzy radial-gradient blobs into it additively for each light, then multiply the whole lightmap over the framebuffer. In Canvas2D that is globalCompositeOperation='lighter' into the offscreen canvas, then 'multiply' onto the main canvas — this alone converts flat tiles into 'lit' environments.
- COLOR GRADING PER AREA is how Eastward and HLD get mood. Pixpil: LUT grading 'plays a very important role in storytelling... adds blue color to the darker area, makes the screen a bit softer'. HLD gives each region a strict color identity (West: dark green grass/red skies/light green crystals; East: vibrant with pink fire), uses complementary blue/orange schemes in bright areas and analogous schemes (blue/indigo/aqua) in dark ones, and 'the visual designers simply love sunbeams, which are placed pretty regularly'.
- TILE VARIATION NUMBERS (Celeste, via aran.ink): 1-4 randomly-swapped variants of each straight edge tile ('solves one of the major problems... they look super repetitive lined up'), ~10 abstract infill/transition tiles between edge and flat fill, 10-15% of infill completely empty, and only 3-5 colors per tileset (1-2 light for edge highlights, 1 mid-tone bulk, 1-2 dark). A complete autotile blob is 47-48 tiles (buildable from ~13 sub-tiles). Slynyrd: base tile must loop on all 4 sides; strive for 'homogenous distribution of visual weight within a tile — if any area has visual dominance the pattern becomes obvious'; mix flat-color tiles with textured ones ('spaces of flat green with textured areas of differing density'); pair busy textures with airy ones at material transitions; dedicated connection tiles between materials.
- OFF-GRID DECOR BREAKS THE GRID MORE THAN MORE TILES DO. CrossCode: 'the graphics have exact placements, so it's not tile by tile' — props/decals are placed at arbitrary pixel positions over the tilemap. Celeste overlays props 'to disrupt their contours'. This is cheaper than authoring more tile variants and reads as hand-crafted.
- AMBIENT PARTICLES ON MULTIPLE DEPTH LAYERS: CrossCode's environment particles 'move around the map and are layered on multiple levels to give a deeper sense of 3D movement (similar to parallax scrolling)', with per-location sets (dust indoors, seagulls outside). Blasphemous stacks multiple parallax background layers moving at different speeds. For an aerial view, particle layers above and below the character plane substitute for horizon parallax.
- WATER (Slynyrd): 2-frame water textures crossfaded through a 50%-opacity blend frame (anime technique) — smooth for almost zero art cost, but 'too fast it feels like noise, too slow it's choppy'; a 6-frame sparkle/shimmer overlay loop with simple linear movement; waterfalls = mouth (bright reflections) + flow (bands that sag and darken downward) + splash synced to the flow; reflections = vertically flipped sprite + per-row horizontal offsets (rows spaced 3+ px) + reduced alpha; and 'if your scene looks bad without animation, it won't look better with it'.
- HEIGHT/DEPTH CUES for top-down elevation: drop shadows cast by cliff edges (Slynyrd: conservative, 'should not exceed a tile in length'); cliff faces drawn with 45-degree angled texture; 1px highlight on top edges adjacent to shadow edges for contrast; darken/desaturate lower tiers (fog-of-depth); blob shadow under airborne entities offset by z-height; sprites shift up-screen with elevation. CrossCode auto-generates cliffs from a height map — tier boundaries are systematic, not hand-tiled.
- ANIMATION MINIMUMS: walk cycles — 4 frames is the readable minimum (contact/passing x2), 6 frames is the smoothness sweet spot (Slynyrd: 'strikes a nice middle ground... sufficiently fluid to stand on its own'), 8 is 'optimum fluid'; >8 is overkill for indie. Typical rates: idle ~6fps, walk ~8fps, attacks 10-12fps. Idles in shipped games are commonly 2 frames with 200-400ms holds plus occasional accents (blink, cloth sway). Blasphemous' 'buttery' feel is high frame counts ONLY on the player and bosses — environments stay nearly static with a few animated accents.
- WHAT READS CHEAP (Derek Yu, Powerhoof, community consensus): mixed pixel densities in one scene ('one off-grid, off-palette asset breaks the spell'); fractional scaling causing shimmer — render at a fixed native resolution (HLD uses 480x270) and integer-scale up; pillow shading; too many similar colors ('pixels blend together and get lost'); naive pure colors instead of hue-shifted ramps; single-pixel-thick limbs. Post-processing: bloom only above a high brightness threshold at low intensity; avoid heavy vignettes that 'crush detail and make everything look like a horror game'; effects are 'seasoning — a pinch, not a whole jar'; a SOFT blurry CRT/LUT combo (Eastward) can work, but hard scanlines usually don't.

## Actionable for Bloodgem

- P1 — SCREEN-SPACE LIGHTMAP (biggest win, ~1 day): keep one offscreen canvas at native resolution; each frame fill it with the area's ambient color (gothic city: cold desaturated blue ~#232a3d; mine: near-black warm brown ~#1c140f), set globalCompositeOperation='lighter' and stamp a radial gradient per light source (lantern/torch/brazier/lava: warm #ffb763 core -> transparent, radius 3-6 tiles; gem/magic: cyan or violet), then draw that canvas over the scene with 'multiply'. Add a second pass drawing the 2-3 brightest lights again directly on the scene with 'lighter' at 5-10% alpha for glow. Flicker torches by jittering radius +/-4% and alpha +/-8% on a slow noise, not per-frame random.
- P1 — PER-AREA COLOR GRADE (poor man's LUT, ~2 hours): after the lightmap, draw one full-screen rect 'multiply' with the area tint plus one 'lighter' rect at low alpha for the warm/cool push. City: multiply #2a3050 at ~25%, lighter #1a0f00 at ~6%. Mine: multiply #33261a at ~20%, lighter #franc =0; use #301505 at ~8% near lava chambers. Lerp these values over ~1s when crossing area boundaries — this is your color script and it makes the two levels read as different worlds for free.
- P2 — AMBIENT PARTICLE SYSTEMS, 2-3 DEPTH LAYERS (CrossCode pattern, ~1 day): (a) Mine dust motes: 25-40 alive per screen, 1-2px, alpha 0.1-0.35 with slow sine twinkle, drift 3-8 px/s with sine wobble, drawn ABOVE the character layer at half count and below at full count for depth. (b) Embers near torches/lava: 8-15 per source area, rise 10-25 px/s with horizontal sine, color ramp #ffd27a->#ff6a33->fade, 1px, die in 1.5-3s. (c) City fog: two large translucent blob/noise textures scrolled at different speeds (~4 px/s and ~9 px/s, directions ~20 degrees apart), each at 6-12% alpha, one under characters and one above at half alpha; add slow drifting 'rain streak' or falling-leaf/ash particles for weather identity. Snap particle draws to whole pixels.
- P2 — DECAL LAYER, OFF-GRID (breaks the 16px grid, ~1 day + art): a sprite layer between ground tiles and entities where decals are placed at arbitrary pixel positions, deliberately straddling tile boundaries: city = cracks, moss patches, blood stains, candle clusters, torn banners, puddles; mine = rubble, ore glints, bones, cart tracks, support-beam shadows. Author 8-12 decal types per level, 1-3 sizes each; scatter so most 5x5-tile regions contain at least one. This is cheaper than more tile variants and kills repetition fastest.
- P3 — TILE VARIANTS + TRANSITIONS (Celeste numbers): for each ground material make 3-4 texture variants of the base tile plus 1 nearly-flat variant, chosen by a hash of tile coords (deterministic, no shimmer on redraw); keep each material to a 3-5 color ramp with hue-shifted (bluer/darker) shadows, never pure gray. Author explicit transition/border tiles wherever two materials meet (cobblestone->dirt, stone->ore vein, floor->void) using the 16-tile edge+corner set minimum, 47-tile blob where materials interlock; pair a busy material with an airy one at every boundary so the seam reads as intentional.
- P3 — HEIGHT-TIER CUES (sells the 3 tiers): (a) cast a 1-tile-max drop shadow from every upper-tier edge onto the tier below (30-40% black, hard pixel edge); (b) 1px light highlight along walkable top edges of cliffs/rooftops; (c) tint tiers relative to the player's tier — tiers below: -10% brightness and -15% saturation per tier; (d) blob shadow under any airborne/falling entity that stays on the ground plane while the sprite offsets up by z; (e) fake parallax: offset tier-2 by ~3% and tier-3 by ~6% of camera position relative to tier-1 when the camera pans — this is the single strongest '2.5D' signal in an aerial view; (f) render cliff faces with 45-degree angled texture, not just darkened top texture.
- P4 — WATER/LIQUID RENDERING: base water = 2 authored 16px texture frames crossfaded via opacity over ~1s each way (draw frame A, draw frame B at animated alpha) — smooth motion from 2 frames; overlay a 6-frame sparkle loop (single bright pixels moving linearly) at 20-30% density; animate 3-4 frame foam/edge tiles at shorelines; for still water (mine flood pools, city fountains) draw a vertically flipped, 35% alpha copy of adjacent sprites with each row x-offset by a slow sine (rows 3+ px apart) for ripple; sewage/blood variants are just the same system with the color ramp swapped.
- P4 — SUNBEAMS/GODRAYS (HLD signature, trivial in Canvas2D): from cathedral windows, grates, and mine shaft openings draw 2-3 overlapping translucent parallelogram gradients ('lighter', 4-8% alpha each, slight width oscillation over ~6s) angled consistently with the area's key light; put 2x dust-mote density inside the beam volume.
- P5 — ANIMATION BUDGET: player walk 6 frames @ ~8fps (4 directions), run 6 @ 10fps, attacks 4-6 keyframes @ 10-12fps with 1-2 smear frames, idle 2 frames with 250-400ms holds + a cloak-sway accent every ~4s; enemies 4-frame walks are fine. Spend the saved frames on environmental accent loops: 4-frame torch flames, 2-frame crossfading water, 3-frame flags/chains, occasional 6-frame bird/rat scurry — a few moving accents per screen read as 'alive' far more than smooth walk cycles do.
- P5 — POST STACK + CHEAP-TELL AVOIDANCE: render everything to a native-resolution canvas (270p-class, e.g. 480x270) and integer-scale to the display with imageSmoothingEnabled=false; all sprites same pixel density, positions floored to whole pixels, NO runtime rotation or fractional scaling of pixel sprites (pre-bake rotations); subtle vignette only (radial gradient, corners max 15-20% darker, 'multiply'); optional 2-3% animated grain in the mine; skip scanlines/CRT entirely or make it a barely-visible soft filter; bloom only from a high threshold (lava, magic gems, stained glass) at low intensity. Order: tiles -> decals -> entities (y-sorted) -> below-fog -> lightmap multiply -> additive glow -> above-fog/particles -> grade tints -> vignette.
