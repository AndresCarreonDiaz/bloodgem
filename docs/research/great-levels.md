# Research: great-levels

## Summary

Great levels — especially FromSoftware's — are loop diagrams before they are geometry: a critical path that folds back on itself through one-way shortcuts unlocked from the far side, anchored by a safe hub, oriented by one dominant visible landmark (a 'weenie'), paced by authored encounter setups that follow a teach/test/twist rhythm, and seasoned with bait-driven secrets and environmental storytelling. Undead Burg, Central Yharnam, and Boletaria 1-1 all follow the same skeleton: descend/branch away from the checkpoint under rising tension, then snap back to it via a kicked ladder, cut chain, or gate opened from behind — converting spatial confusion into a single 'aha' of comprehension. Anor Londo shows the complementary trick: after sustained claustrophobic tension, break the pattern with light, scale, and a vista whose destination you will physically reach. For Bloodgem's aerial 2.5D grid, the direct translation is: use the 3 height tiers as the verticality engine (see-down vistas, one-way drops, tier-crossing shortcuts), give each level 3-4 districts with distinct tile palettes/music/enemy rosters, and script every encounter as breadcrumb, ambush lesson, or breather — never as filler.

## Key findings

- LOOP-BACK SHORTCUTS ARE THE CORE UNIT, AND THEY UNLOCK FROM THE FAR SIDE: Dark Souls 1's kicked ladder from Undead Parish down into Firelink Shrine, Demon's Souls 1-1's cut chains and pulled lever that open gates back to the level entrance, Bloodborne's gate that turns sprawling Central Yharnam into a tight loop back to the first lamp. The pattern: the player travels a long risky arc away from the checkpoint, then opens a one-way connection from the FAR side — the reward is retroactive comprehension ('the map folds into itself'). Shortcuts must be one-way until unlocked (ladder kicked from above, bar lifted from behind, elevator called from the bottom) or the loop leaks.
- MIYAZAKI: 'THE MAP DESIGN DICTATED EVERYTHING ELSE' — layout/connection graph is locked before art or encounters. FromSoft draws the region connection map first (design docs show cross-sectional sketches: Blighttown under Firelink, Anor Londo above Sen's Fortress), then fills in. A level is a graph of checkpoint nodes, gate edges, and one-way edges; tiles come last.
- HUB DESIGN: Firelink Shrine works because it is (a) an 'intimate space' — small, enclosed, visually warm, musically distinct, safe; (b) has 3+ spokes radiating to areas of different difficulty (Undead Burg up, New Londo down, Catacombs across, Blighttown deep); (c) multiple areas RETURN to it via elevators/ladders discovered later. The emotional beat is 'fight through hell, take an elevator, and land back in the safety net.' Bloodborne shows the hybrid: a detached hub (Hunter's Dream) still works if the field areas are locally interconnected like DS1.
- VISTA/WEENIE: distant landmarks that orient and promise. The Disney 'weenie' (a structure taller than everything, drawing visitors) is the standard: Half-Life 2 frames the Citadel the moment you exit the train station; DS1 lets you SEE Blighttown from Firelink's graveyard cliff long before you suffer it; Central Yharnam shows later levels (the Great Bridge, Cathedral Ward) from the get-go. Rule: what you show, the player must eventually stand on — arrival pays off the promise, and looking BACK down at the path you climbed is the second payoff ('you're staring at the winding path you took on the way up').
- ANOR LONDO ARRIVAL = PATTERN BREAK AS CLIMAX: after Sen's Fortress (dark, cramped, trap-dense, held-breath tension), the game deliberately breaks its own interconnection and palette — sudden sunlight, monumental scale, open prospect space, a bonfire immediately. The vista lands because of CONTRAST with what preceded it: rarefaction and relief after compression. Arrivals are earned by the corridor before them.
- ENCOUNTERS FOLLOW TEACH/TEST/TWIST AND ENEMIES ARE BREADCRUMBS (Undead Burg case study): first fight is 2 visible melee + a 3rd placed deeper who 'pulls attention over the bridge' toward the next area (enemy as wayfinding); then a 4th hidden in a dark doorway (teaches corner-checking: 'if you don't check your corners, we will mess you up'); then an escalated 'predictable ambush' of 4 hanging zombies that a smart player can foil. Compound arenas layer one elevated ranged unit (crossbowman in tower creating a firing line) + melee rushing from breakable barricades + one delayed reinforcement from the rear. Every encounter has a job: teach, test, twist, or breadcrumb — never filler.
- SUPER METROID'S INVISIBLE HAND: enemies as tutors and economy. Etecoons demonstrate the wall-jump by doing it in front of you; space pirates that only die to missiles showcase the new weapon AND drop missiles so you can keep using it; harmless fireflies teach that not everything is hostile. Repetition plants tool associations (three identical grapple-block caverns right after getting the Grapple Beam so the later puzzle reads instantly). Map rooms reveal most of an area's skeleton but leave one connection missing to hint at secrets; one-way 'shafts of no return' focus attention and control backtracking.
- PACING = DENSITY CURVE + SPACE-TYPE ALTERNATION: souls levels start branch-dense (exploration rewarded while player is fresh) and streamline to near-linear before the boss (focus as fatigue sets in). Alternate narrow claustrophobic spaces with open prospect spaces to create emotional rhythm; a claustrophobic corridor immediately before a huge boss arena amplifies the arena. Direction carries emotion: descending = dread and uncertainty (Blighttown, the further down the more unreachable Firelink feels), ascending = triumph (Anor Londo). Breathers (merchant nooks, safe rooms, vista ledges) placed after tension spikes.
- SECRETS/REWARD PSYCHOLOGY: glowing corpse-loot is deliberate bait — visible from a vista, it pulls players off the critical path and into ambushes or one-way drops (risk is the price of the item). Best items sit behind precarious jumps, optional elite enemies, or illusory walls; illusory walls are signposted by subtle irregularity (a dead-end that shouldn't be one, a corpse facing a blank wall, a texture seam). The Undead Burg merchant hidden under breakable crates mid-level teaches 'break everything'; Central Yharnam's Gascoigne's-daughter window dispenses the Music Box, a boss-weakening item found only by explorers — exploration converts into combat power, not just trinkets.
- SUB-AREA IDENTITY: one level = several named districts, each with its own palette, soundscape, prop set, and enemy roster. Hollow Knight's City of Tears is the model: perpetual rain particles, muffled piano with mourning vocals, art-nouveau signage in pastel against dark stone — mood is built from ONE dominant environmental system (the rain) plus music, and the signage doubles as wayfinding. Bramasole's souls-level taxonomy: Town (dense connected buildings, circular streets — Undead Burg), Dungeon (winding, directional — Blighttown/mines), Cathedral (single vertical building with looping floors). Space vocabulary: narrow / prospect / intimate / depressed (threats from above) / elevated (commanding view).
- ENVIRONMENTAL STORYTELLING BEATS: collapsed bridges, sealed gates seen from the wrong side, corpse tableaux, blood trails (Doom) and destruction trails (BioShock) that double as path guidance. Boletaria 1-1's Ostrava calling for help from a parapet, Yharnam's locked doors with hostile voices behind them — NPCs embedded IN the geometry make the level a place. Late-game re-use extends a level's life: Iosefka's Clinic reopens from the other side after Forbidden Woods, recontextualizing the very first room of the game.
- GATING VOCABULARY: hard gates (key/boss-locked doors), soft gates (hazards that slow but don't stop), psychological gates (fog doors trigger anxiety and make players 'procrastinate through exploration' — which is exactly when they find secrets), and one-way edges (drops, kicked ladders) that enforce flow direction without walls. Undead Burg's 'fog gate joke' (empty room, then the Hellkite dragon strafes the bridge) shows gates can also set up scripted spectacle.
- AUDIT CHECKLIST — WHAT A GREAT LEVEL HAS THAT A RECTANGULAR GRID LEVEL LACKS: (1) one dominant landmark visible from most of the level, with routes oriented toward it; (2) at least 2-3 loops closed by far-side one-way shortcuts back to a checkpoint — zero pure out-and-back corridors; (3) vertical overlap: paths that cross above/below each other so players SEE earlier/later areas; (4) one-way edges (drops, ladders, barred doors) shaping flow; (5) a density curve: branchy start, linear boss approach; (6) alternation of tight and open spaces instead of uniform corridor width; (7) 3+ sub-areas with distinct palette/music/enemies instead of homogeneous texture; (8) every encounter authored as teach/test/twist/breadcrumb with named roles (puller, ambusher, overwatch, reinforcement) instead of evenly sprinkled enemies; (9) a denial-and-reveal: the goal shown early, reached late, ideally revisitable to look back; (10) baited secrets signposted by irregularities, with reward proportional to risk; (11) an intimate safe node (hub/bonfire) contrasting with dangerous prospect spaces; (12) at least one arrival/pattern-break moment where palette, light, and music change together; (13) environmental story beats readable without text; (14) late-game recontextualization — a door in the first room that only opens from the other side, hours later.

## Actionable for Bloodgem

- Design each Bloodgem level as a node graph BEFORE placing tiles: nodes = checkpoints, boss, shortcut gates, secrets; edges tagged two-way / one-way / locked-far-side. Rule of thumb from DS1/DeS 1-1: 2-3 far-side shortcut unlocks per level, each cutting a 3-5 minute arc down to <30 seconds back to the checkpoint. Implement as tile-edge flags: 'one-way drop' (tier N -> N-1 only), 'kick-ladder' (unlocks two-way after triggering from top), 'barred door' (opens only from the far side), 'elevator' (callable only after first ride).
- Use the 3 height tiers as your interconnection engine, not just decoration. Gothic city: tier 1 = streets/canals, tier 2 = rooftops and footbridges, tier 3 = cathedral ramparts. Make routes physically overlap: a tier-2 bridge crossing above a tier-1 plaza the player fought through an hour earlier. Mine: invert the emotion — descending tiers get darker (lower ambient light, tighter camera/vignette, dripping SFX) so depth reads as dread, and the run back UP to the elevator reads as relief (DS1 Blighttown principle).
- Aerial-view vista substitute: when the player stands on tier 2/3, render lower tiers below with darkened/desaturated tint but keep loot glows and enemy silhouettes visible — that is your 'see the path you'll take / the path you took' moment in top-down. Also add 2-3 authored 'ledge vista' tiles that pan/reveal the camera toward the level's landmark and unrevealed map geometry (denial-and-reveal).
- Give each level one weenie: gothic city = a multi-tile cathedral occupying tier 3 with a distinctive amber blood-lantern glow, visible (even as silhouette past the fog-of-war edge) from most streets; orient main streets and lamp-post rows as leading lines toward it. Mine = a giant elevator headframe / glowing bloodgem seam visible down a long central shaft. The final boss should be AT the weenie so every glimpse is a promise the game keeps.
- Script one Anor Londo arrival per level: e.g., the mine's elevator rises into the cathedral interior — hard palette break (mine browns/blacks -> cold blue-grey + amber windows), music change, brief camera hold, checkpoint immediately. Precede it with the tightest, most oppressive corridor in the level; the contrast IS the payoff.
- Adopt Undead Burg encounter recipes with named roles: (a) opener = 2 visible melee + 1 'puller' placed deeper along the intended route; (b) corner lesson = 1 ambusher just outside camera/FOV behind a door or wall edge, placed early and repeated with escalation (1 hidden -> 2 at a ledge -> 4 foilable); (c) compound arena = 1 ranged overwatch on a HIGHER TIER covering an open plaza + melee rushing from breakable barricades + 1 delayed reinforcement from behind; (d) introduce each new enemy type ALONE in a low-stakes room before mixing it. Never sprinkle enemies evenly across the grid.
- Teach through enemies, Metroid-style: the ranged mine enemy drops the throwable that counters armored foes; a harmless critter in the city teaches that some creatures are neutral; if you add a movement mechanic (ledge-drop attack, ladder slide), have an NPC or creature demonstrate it visibly like the Etecoons.
- Pace by density: first third of each level is branch-dense (3-4 way intersections, optional loot loops), final approach to the boss is a single streamlined path with a fog-door equivalent (shimmering veil tile) plus a breather/stock-up nook right before it. After every shortcut unlock or checkpoint, place a low-threat intimate space: NPC window, lore corpse, vista ledge.
- Hub: make the checkpoint hub of each level an 'intimate space' — small circular room, unique warm palette, own music loop, no enemies ever, 3+ exits at different tiers. Have at least two late-level routes dump back INTO it (elevator from mine depths, kicked ladder from city rooftops) for the Firelink 'safety net' beat.
- Secrets on a 16px grid: breakable/illusory wall tiles signposted by subtle irregularity (1-2px crack variant tile, different mortar color, a corpse sprite facing the wall, a dead-end that violates the district's street logic). Use glowing corpse loot as authored bait: visible from an upper tier, reachable only via a one-way drop into an ambush or hazard — risk priced into the reward. Put the level's best item behind an optional elite, not on the critical path.
- Sub-area identity: split the gothic city into 3-4 districts (e.g., Market Rows, Canal/Sewer Under-tier, Chapel Ward) each with its own tile palette, prop set, 1-2 signature enemies, and music layer; give one district a City of Tears-style persistent environmental system — Canvas2D rain particle layer + puddle/reflection tiles + muffled melancholic loop. Mine districts: timbered shafts, crystal-vein caverns (bloodgem glow as light source), flooded depths. District transitions should be readable within one screen.
- Environmental storytelling beats per level (pick 4-6): a barricaded door with corpses piled on the defender side; a sealed gate the player sees from the wrong side an hour before opening it; a Gascoigne's-daughter-style NPC behind a lit window who gives a boss-counter item (your Music Box equivalent); a blood trail leading to a secret; miners' tools abandoned mid-swing near where the horror begins.
- Map as reward: a findable/purchasable district map item that fills the minimap skeleton (Super Metroid map room) but deliberately omits one connection — the omission itself hints at the secret route between the city and the mine.
- Run the 14-point audit checklist (see key findings) against both levels before content-complete; the failure mode of a tile-grid game is exactly the rectangular-corridor pattern the checklist catches — no landmark, no loops, no vertical overlap, uniform density, unauthored enemy sprinkle.
