# Research: topdown-souls-design

## Summary

Verticality in top-down/2.5D games is a solved engineering problem with a well-documented canonical pattern, and CrossCode is the best-documented reference. Entities carry a true 3D position — x/y are ground-plane coordinates, z is height — and the renderer fakes elevation by drawing sprites at screenY = y - z while a shadow blob stays pinned at the ground y (this is exactly The Waking Cloak's GameMaker recipe: `draw_sprite(..., x, y - z)`, a zSpeed gravity variable, and depth-sorting actors by their feet/bbox_bottom). Maps are authored as a stack of discrete height "levels": CrossCode's open-source map editor shows the exact JSON schema — `levels: {height:number}[]`, a `masterLevel` ground index, and each Background/Collision layer bound to a level index, with entities placed at `level: number | {level, offset}`. Combat interoperates with height via a simple tier rule (in CrossCode you can hit/be hit only on the same plane or one tier above/below), pathfinding uses one nav grid per height level with hand-annotated jump-up edges and automatically computed jump-down edges, and one-way drop-downs are just ledge tiles that are solid in one direction. Occlusion when the player walks behind higher geometry is handled by fading occluding tiles or drawing a silhouette pass.

On souls-like tuning, hard frame data is scarcer than expected but the patterns are consistent: dashes are short (~0.25-0.45s), i-frames are front-loaded and cancelled the moment the player attacks out of the dash (Hades' explicit rule), and games that shipped without dodge i-frames (Hyper Light Drifter at launch) had to patch them in due to player revolt — then trim them back for challenge. Eldest Souls (the most Bloodborne-adjacent top-down boss-rush) uses a 3-charge dash with 3.5s per-charge refill plus a "Perfect Dodge" last-moment-timing reward signalled by a green character flash, and its Bloodburst mechanic (lifesteal window after landing a charged hit) is a direct translation of Bloodborne's rally/aggression reward to top-down. Unsighted's parry is notable for its forgiveness ladder: a perfect parry stuns long enough to punish, but a missed window still "reflects" the attack and stumbles the enemy, and the game ships an in-game parry trainer.

Telegraphing in top-down view converges on a two-channel language: (1) a wind-up on the enemy body (anticipation pose, charge particles, "vacuum-cleaner-in-reverse" rising sound, bright flash right before the active frames) and (2) a ground-projected AoE indicator — Hades' "big red circles saying get away from here." The key documented failure mode is breaking your own color language (Hades II's Chronos fight was criticized for yellow telegraphs on a yellow stage after the whole game taught red). For combat juice, the numbers are concrete: ~50ms of hit-stop is the research-backed sweet spot (perception threshold is ~100ms), Capcom beat 'em ups used 4-10 frames scaled by attack weight and made the *victim* freeze ~2 frames longer than the attacker, and Vlambeer's Art of Screenshake gives the full ordered checklist (enemy knockback, permanence/corpses, camera lerp, screenshake, player knockback, sleep frames) — all trivially implementable in a custom Canvas engine.

## Key findings

- CrossCode entity physics (Radical Fish Games dev blog, fetched): every entity has pos {x,y,z}, velocity {x,y,z}, accelDir, plus 'maxVel: 300, accelSpeed: 1.0, friction: 1.0, airFriction: 0.2' — 'the x and y coordinate are the ground coordinates and z the height coordinate (positive z means above ground)'; horizontal movement physics only touch ground coords, and air friction (0.2 vs 1.0) applies while z > ground.
- CrossCode combat-across-height rule (Steam discussion summary): 'you are generally able to hit and be hit when the enemy is on the same plane, or one tier above or below' — a simple tier-delta check, not continuous z collision.
- CrossCode map JSON schema (fetched from crosscode-map-editor GitHub, cross-code-map.ts): `CrossCodeMap { name; levels: { height: number }[]; mapWidth; mapHeight; masterLevel: number; entities: MapEntity[]; layer: MapLayer[]; ... }`; `MapLayer { type; level; distance: number; tilesize; moveSpeed: Point; data: number[][] }` where type is 'Background' or 'Collision' and each layer is bound to a level index; `MapEntity { type; x; y; level: number | { level: number, offset: number }; settings }`. Tiles are 16x16 px; masterLevel is the index of the ground level.
- CrossCode pathfinding with verticality (Radical Fish blog, fetched): one NavMap per height level; nodes span multiple tiles; level transitions are 'jump-up-edges' (hand-marked with arrow tiles) while 'jump-down-edges... are computed automatically and don't require any special tiles'; 'AirNodes' represent jumpable gaps between platforms; 'we only need to find a path that leads the entity toward the right walls and edges — the jumping happens automatically.'
- The Waking Cloak z-height recipe (fetched, GameMaker but engine-agnostic): draw at `(x, y - z)`; shadow stays at ground y; GROUND constant = 96px with cliff height objects in 8px increments; gravity: `if (z > groundZ) zSpeed -= gravitySpeed; ... z += zSpeed`; collision must collect ALL overlapping colliders into a list then test z-range overlap (`z + height` vs `z + height`) because naive 2D collision returns only the first hit; depth-sort cliffs by -bbox_top and actors by -bbox_bottom (feet position).
- Grayscale heightmap authoring (Yet Another Zelda Fangame devlog, fetched): 'Each map in the game is represented as an image file with shades of grey representing various heights'; converted to a 10x10px-cell tilemap of floor/wall/outline; terrain pre-generated in-editor (Godot @tool) rather than at runtime; elevation data doubles as input for shadow casting with a movable sun. Dev's warning: fake-3D-in-2D 'is a pain to make. My rendition of it is still full of bugs and oddities.'
- One-way drop-down ledges (Zelda-style tilemap collision, Medium/Abachi): 'One-way ledges allow movement only when motion comes from the allowed direction and reject the other direction by treating the tile as solid for that frame.'
- Occlusion handling when player is behind tall geometry (Red Blob isometric outline experiments + Unity forum): either alpha-fade the occluding wall tiles, or render a silhouette: use the character rendered to a texture with a mask to detect hidden pixels and draw a tinted silhouette for the hidden portion; Red Blob's outline approach draws surface IDs to a buffer and edge-detects ID changes in a post-pass.
- Hades dash (Fandom wiki + Steam threads, wiki fetch blocked with HTTP 402): the whole dash animation is invulnerable BUT 'the invincibility is lost immediately if you take any other action, such as using a dash attack or dropping your cast' — community test: 'I dashed through exploding little chariots, no damage. I dash attacked through them (not at them), damage.' I-frames are weighted 'more at the start of the dash'; the very end is punishable. No official frame count was ever published; Supergiant devs denied stealth-nerfing it ('at least not intentionally'). Hades II made the old Swift Runner the default dash: 'starts more quickly but has fewer i-frames and less distance', and shows i-frame duration diegetically — 'mel's entire body changes color for a moment.'
- Hades adjacent numbers that ARE documented (GameFAQs weapon/boon guide): Hermes' Hyper Sprint = '0.5 seconds of sturdy and 100% faster movement after dashing'; Dash Nova grants Sturdy 0.8s; Sturdy = 30% damage reduction + no interruption (a poise analog you can copy).
- Hyper Light Drifter dash history (Steam announcement 'The Invincibility Patch' + wiki Patch History): the game SHIPPED with zero dash i-frames, added 'frames of invincibility... to dash' in a patch after player feedback, then a later patch REDUCED them to preserve 'fairness in design while maintaining challenge'. Steam-forum testing places the i-frames 'a few frames in the middle' of the dash, not at startup. Chain-dash input window from community macro data: a 270ms press cycle works (50ms pressed, 220ms released); '250ms being too tight and 300ms becoming unreliable.'
- Death's Door roll (ResetEra OT + Acid Nerve interviews): 'The i-frames on the roll are very generous' but 'somewhat inconsistent and you can oversample inputs when attempting to dodge roll' (input-eating bug class to avoid); Acid Nerve 'prioritized maintaining responsiveness by ensuring attacks and dodgerolls execute within very few frames of button presses'; design target was the 'fast arcadey flow of A Link to the Past (or Zelda but with intense combat)' rather than FromSoft pacing; they deliberately dropped corpse-run souls loss — Fenn: 'there were a lot of games coming out where you lose all your souls when you die, and I was just getting fatigued by it'; deflected projectiles deal double the damage of the deflecting swing; melee hits recharge ranged magic (copied from Hyper Light Drifter).
- Eldest Souls dodge economy (ScreenRant combat guide + reviews): three dash charges, 'each dash charge refilling in 3.5 seconds'; Perfect Dodge = dodging at the last possible moment — 'if the character flashes green as they go through the attack, the player was successful and takes no damage'; Winter Fangs talent: after a perfect dodge 'your next dash within 1.5 seconds cost no stamina'; Bloodburst = landing a charged attack grants a temporary buff that 'allows you to regain health with each attack that follows' (explicit Bloodborne rally translation); Lightbringer variant: 22 damage ray + 40% move speed for 3 seconds.
- Unsighted parry (GameRant/TV Tropes): perfect parry stuns the enemy 'long enough to charge a spin attack'; a MISSED parry window still 'reflects' the attack and makes the enemy stumble (soft-failure forgiveness); some attacks require two parries to stun; all combat actions cost regenerating stamina; perfect reload minigame refills the gun AND recharges stamina; the game ships a dedicated Parry Training vendor so players can practice risk-free.
- Tunic (reviews): stamina-based souls combat where an EMPTY stamina bar makes you take increased damage (soft punishment instead of action lockout); documented isometric readability flaw: 'The camera angle means some attacks come from off-screen' — a warning for any top-down souls-like about enemy aggro/leash ranges vs viewport.
- Morbid: The Seven Acolytes (Dread Central/Third Coast reviews) is the closest existing 'top-down Bloodborne': 'a top-down action game with Bloodborne vibes' with eldritch bosses, simplified Souls combat, ~8 hours long — reviewers praised aesthetic/bosses but called combat 'pretty simplified', i.e. the niche of a mechanically deep top-down Bloodborne is still open.
- Telegraphing doctrine (Gamedeveloper.com 'Enemy Attacks and Telegraphing', fetched): 'If players don't understand the questions they are being asked, they actually can not play your game'; every attack needs a pre-attack delay ('Okay, here I come. I'm gonna attack now… Can you avoid my damage?'); telegraphs should layer 'animation, sound effects, voice-over, visual effects, and sometimes even force-feedback'; canonical projectile charge-up: 'A ball of particles accumulates around the barrel of the weapon, you hear a kind of vacuum-cleaner-in-reverse sound, and then there's a bright flash. Only after all that does the projectile let fly.'
- Hades telegraph color language (Steam Hades II threads): the game consistently uses 'big, red circles saying get away from here' for ground AoE; the Chronos boss was criticized because 'it's yellow everywhere with yellow stage, yellow attacks, yellow enemies, and yellow telegraphs' — breaking the color language the game taught is a documented player-facing failure.
- Hit-stop numbers (Oreate AI research summary + arXiv impact-feel study): human perception window for instantaneous events is ~100ms; 'around .05-second (50 millisecond) duration yields significant improvement, with visuals appearing more natural'; scale duration with damage; overuse 'compromises fluidity, resulting in a mechanical feel'; the arXiv study found hit stop, sound coherence, and camera control are the three features that most strongly influence impact feel and weakness in any one 'may ruin players' impact feel.'
- Capcom beat 'em up hitstop frame data (Shane Sicienski blog, fetched, 60fps arcade): Final Fight = universal 6 frames on every attack; Captain Commando ~8; Knights of the Round 6 ground / 7 air; Warriors of Fate varies per move (jab 5f, air 4f, thrown enemies 0f); The Punisher uses ASYMMETRIC hitstop — attacker/victim: jab 6/8, cross 8/10, combo ender 9/11, air 10/11 — freezing the victim ~2 frames longer than the attacker keeps the attacker's flow while selling impact.
- Vlambeer 'The Art of Screenshake' ordered technique list (fetched transcript page, with talk timestamps): basic animation+sound → lower enemy hp → higher rate of fire → more enemies → bigger bullets → muzzle flash → faster bullets → less accuracy → impact effects → hit animation → enemy knockback (12:42) → permanence/corpses (13:18) → camera lerp (14:49) → camera position toward aim (15:27) → screen shake (16:36) → player knockback (17:15) → sleep/hit-stop 'momentary pause when hitting enemy... a frame or a couple of frames... sweet spot before players become conscious of the freeze' (18:06) → gun delay + kickback animations → 'meaning (player can die)'.
- Sprite depth sorting for jumping actors (GameDev.net GameMaker thread + Godot docs): set draw depth from the FEET y-position (y + spriteHeight / bbox_bottom), draw at (x, y - z), and keep the shadow at (x, y) — 'the y axis does double duty' for depth sorting while z only offsets rendering; Godot formalizes this as the YSort node.

## Actionable for Bloodgem

- Adopt the CrossCode entity model verbatim: `pos: {x, y, z}` in world units, `vel: {x, y, z}`, ground friction ~1.0 vs airFriction ~0.2 applied only when z > groundHeight(x,y). Movement input only accelerates x/y; z is owned by a gravity integrator (zVel -= g*dt; z += zVel*dt; clamp to groundZ). This cleanly separates dodge/knockback physics from falls and drop-downs.
- Copy CrossCode's map schema for Bloodgem's level JSON: `{ levels: [{height: 0}, {height: 32}, ...], masterLevel: 0, layers: [{type: 'Background'|'Collision', level: i, data: number[][]}], entities: [{type, x, y, level: {level, offset}}] }`. One collision grid PER height level, 16px tiles. This gives you stairs/cliffs/bridges for free: a bridge is a Background+Collision layer pair on level 2 drawn after level 0's.
- Renderer: single painter's-algorithm pass — sort all drawables by (levelIndex, feetY) and draw sprites at `screenY = worldY - z` (add a projection constant if you want a steeper 'aerial' look, e.g. screenY = y - 0.75*z). Draw a soft ellipse shadow at (x, worldY) whose radius shrinks and alpha fades with z — this is THE height-readability cue in every reference game; ship it before anything else vertical.
- Cross-height combat rule: hits connect only when |attackerTier - targetTier| <= 1 (CrossCode's rule). Store tier on hitboxes, not pixel z-ranges — cheaper and more predictable for players.
- One-way drop-downs: mark ledge edge tiles with a direction bit; treat as solid unless approached from the high side, then play a hop: lock input ~150-200ms, tween z down with gravity, dust puff + shadow growing to meet the sprite on landing. Jump-down edges can be auto-derived from the height grid at load time (CrossCode does exactly this for AI nav too).
- Enemy pathfinding: build one coarse nav grid per height level; auto-compute jump-down edges at every ledge; only hand-annotate jump-up points if enemies need to climb. Souls-like enemies mostly don't need to traverse levels — leashing them to their tier is authentic and cheap.
- Dodge tuning starting point (60fps): total roll 0.35-0.45s; i-frames frames 1-15 (~0.25s), front-loaded like Hades, vulnerable recovery tail of ~6-10 frames; CANCEL remaining i-frames the instant the player buffers an attack out of the dodge (Hades' rule — preserves risk in aggressive play). Buffer inputs ~100-150ms and consume exactly one dodge per press to avoid Death's Door's documented 'oversampled inputs' inconsistency. Do NOT ship without dodge i-frames — Hyper Light Drifter had to patch them in.
- Consider Eldest Souls' dash economy over a Dark Souls stamina bar for boss fights: 3 dash charges, ~3.5s refill each, plus a Perfect Dodge (last-100-150ms timing) that flashes the player sprite green/white and refunds the charge or grants a free dash within 1.5s. It reads perfectly in top-down and rewards Bloodborne-style aggression.
- Parry (your gun-parry analog): active window ~10-12 frames (~170-200ms) as a start; on success, freeze both actors ~8 frames, flash, and stagger the enemy long enough for a guaranteed 'visceral' charged attack. Copy Unsighted's forgiveness ladder: a near-miss still deflects/stumbles without the full stun, and add a practice arena NPC. Rally mechanic: after taking damage, a decaying orange segment on the HP bar is recoverable by hitting anything within ~3-4s — plus an Eldest-Souls-style Bloodburst (lifesteal window after a charged hit) if you want aggression rewarded even at full HP.
- Standardize ONE telegraph language and never break it (the Chronos lesson): red ground shapes = unavoidable-by-blocking, dodge out; yellow flash on the enemy body = parryable melee; every attack gets (a) anticipation pose 300-600ms scaled by damage, (b) 2-frame white body flash right before active frames, (c) rising pitch SFX, (d) for AoE: a red circle/cone outline on the GROUND plane (project it at worldY, not screenY, so it reads under airborne enemies) that fills radially in sync with the wind-up timer — trivial with two Canvas arc() calls (outline + clipped fill).
- Hit-juice budget per hit (60fps Canvas): hit-stop by skipping entity updates (not rAF) — 3 frames (~50ms) light, 6 frames medium, 8-10 frames heavy/kill; make the VICTIM freeze ~2 frames longer than the attacker (The Punisher's trick); 1-2 frame white flash on the victim sprite (pre-render a white-tinted sprite copy or use ctx.globalCompositeOperation='source-atop'); knockback impulse on every hit (add to vel.x/y, let friction eat it); screenshake as a decaying random offset applied to the camera transform (amplitude ~2-6px scaled by damage, exponential decay ~0.85/frame, clamp total); camera lerp toward a point between player and lock-on target; permanence — leave corpses and blood decals on the ground layer (cheap: draw once into an offscreen 'decal' canvas per map) for the Bloodborne mood.
- Occlusion readability: when the player's tier is lower than a foreground layer covering their screen position, either drop that layer's tiles to ~40% alpha in a radius around the player, or draw a flat-color player silhouette on top (draw the player sprite again tinted via a second offscreen canvas). Also respect Tunic's documented flaw: never let enemies attack from off-screen — clamp aggro/attack ranges to ~80% of viewport distance.
- Study order for feel references: Hades for dash cancel rules and red-circle telegraphs; Eldest Souls for dash-charge economy and Bloodburst; Unsighted for parry forgiveness; Death's Door for responsiveness-first animation (act within 1-3 frames of input, put weight in the follow-through, not the startup); Morbid: The Seven Acolytes as the direct 'top-down Bloodborne' competitor whose main criticism was shallow combat — Bloodgem's opportunity is depth (rally + parry + verticality), not aesthetics alone.
