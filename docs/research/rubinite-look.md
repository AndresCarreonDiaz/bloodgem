# Research: rubinite-look

## Summary

"Rubinite" is unambiguous: it is a dark-fantasy 2D pixel-art boss-rush action game by Cup Dog Games (a four-person Taiwanese studio founded in 2021), published with GameWorks Ventures, releasing on Steam July 23, 2026 for Windows and macOS (Steam app 1845250; demo app 2320010). No other game or notable media shares the name — "rubinite" otherwise only appears as an obscure ruby-like gem trade name. The demo is one of Steam's best-received Next Fest demos ever for its size: "Overwhelmingly Positive," 96% of 1,276 reviews, and GamesRadar covered it as "the top-rated Soulslike in Steam Next Fest... turns out it's not a Soulslike but a boss rush with amazing combat." You play Ruby, a red-hooded, bunny-eared princess fleeing the Scarlet Kingdom, fighting only bosses ("Blood Recipients") using a hold-to-observe "Focus" gauge that stacks up to three marks, then landing a critical thrust into the boss's exposed "Pure Blood Crystal." Perfect dodges instantly fill the gauge; standing closer fills it faster. Its stated inspirations are Titan Souls (weak-point core), Sekiro/Dark Souls III (combat timing), and Hollow Knight (character silhouette).

Visually, Rubinite is a top-down/3-4 overhead pixel-art game in the Titan Souls–Hyper Light Drifter lineage — Steam tags it "Top-Down, 2D, Pixel Graphics"; vgtimes calls it an "isometric boss rush." The camera is not straight vertical top-down: characters are drawn in three-quarter view on a dominant ground plane, with arenas read from above. The art operates on deliberate contrast at two levels. First, fidelity contrast: "a cute pixel art style during gameplay that becomes much more intricate during cutscenes or menus" (Punished Backlog) — small, chunky, readable gameplay sprites versus high-detail illustrated cutscene/menu art. Second, tonal contrast: cute Ghibli-esque hub scenes and a bunny-hooded heroine set against "decapitations, executions, and blood spatter," "the pixel art style clashing wonderfully with the guts and gore Ruby leaves in her wake." The palette is dark fantasy built around scarlet/ruby reds (Scarlet Kingdom, blood crystals, Ruby's red hood) over bleak desaturated environments — vgtimes describes "dark pixel-art" and "minimalist animations creating a bleak yet elegant atmosphere." Lighting is dynamic 2D point-lighting over the pixel art: a Steam demo review specifically bug-reported "lighting sources flickering abnormally around lanterns and fireplaces," confirming real-time flickering light sources rather than baked lighting.

What makes it land is polish and feel rather than raw fidelity: "exceptional sound design and animation work gives that perfect dodge such oomph that it hits the brain the same way a parry does in Sekiro" (Punished Backlog), instant restarts, consistent speed, and intuitive controls. Reviewers' one animation criticism is instructive: Ruby's turning "feels kinda off... too rough, maybe adding some in-between animations could make it feel more fluid." The dev interview (UK Anime Network) reveals their process: bosses are prototyped as a blocky "tofu man" until combat is fun, and only then skinned with art; Yi-Xuan Song is the sole lead artist handling character design, environments, and visuals. For Bloodgem, "looks like Rubinite" should mean: 3/4 overhead camera on a pixel-art ground plane, small readable hero sprite vs. huge multi-tile bosses, a scarlet-on-darkness palette where red is the reserved signal color, dynamic flickering point lights, drop shadows for depth, minimal in-combat HUD with lavish illustrated menus, and animation budget concentrated on the dodge/parry moment.

## Key findings

- Identity: Rubinite is by Cup Dog Games (4 Taiwanese university grads, founded 2021, met at Taiwan's Vision Get Wild competition), co-published with GameWorks Ventures; Steam app 1845250, demo app 2320010; launches July 23, 2026 on Windows + macOS with 8 languages. No other game shares the name.
- Demo reception: 'Overwhelmingly Positive' — 96% of 1,276 Steam reviews (recent: 86% of 45). GamesRadar headline: 'I played the top-rated Soulslike in Steam Next Fest... turns out it's not a Soulslike but a boss rush with amazing combat.' vgtimes: it stands out 'not for its scale but for its polish — featuring instant restarts, consistent speed, intuitive controls, and a satisfying sense of impact.'
- Camera: top-down/3-4 overhead pixel-art perspective. Steam tags: '2D, Top-Down, Pixel Graphics, Dark Fantasy, Anime, Medieval, Souls-like, Boss Rush, Difficult, Female Protagonist, Action RPG.' vgtimes calls it a 'tightly designed isometric boss rush.' Its declared core inspiration is Titan Souls, which uses the same 3/4 overhead view — not straight vertical top-down; characters drawn in three-quarter view on a visible ground plane.
- Art style has two-tier fidelity: 'a cute pixel art style during gameplay that becomes much more intricate during cutscenes or menus' (Punished Backlog). Steam community reviewer: 'really decent pixel art, great scenarios and amazing cutscenes.' UK Anime Network: 'The pixel art is gorgeous. Let's get that out of the way.'
- Tonal contrast is the signature: 'a pair of cute bunny ears on her hood with a fist clenching a kitchen knife at her side'; hub areas are 'almost Ghibli-esque' while fights feature 'decapitations, executions, and blood spatter... the pixel art style clashing wonderfully with the guts and gore Ruby leaves in her wake' (Punished Backlog).
- Palette: dark fantasy dominated by scarlet/ruby red (Scarlet Kingdom, 'Pure Blood Crystals', Ruby's red hood) over bleak desaturated environments; vgtimes: 'dark pixel-art' with 'minimalist animations creating a bleak yet elegant atmosphere.' Dev site: 'exquisitely pixel art' capturing 'the majestic and epic essence of the Scarlet Kingdom.'
- Lighting is dynamic, not baked: a Steam demo review bug-reported 'lighting sources flickering abnormally around lanterns and fireplaces' — real-time 2D point lights with flicker rendered over the pixel art.
- Combat/Focus frame data (from videogamelizard + Punished Backlog): hold R2 to Focus — Ruby loses free movement (dodge-roll only) while an observation gauge fills; fill rate scales with proximity ('the further away Ruby is, the slower the gauge fills'); gauge completion places a mark, stackable to 3; a 'perfect' dodge instantly fills the gauge; at full marks you target the boss's Pure Blood Crystal for a devastating Critical Strike. 'The longer you wait to strike, the more damage you'll deal, but a longer wait is more chances for your foe to reset your charge-up' (UK Anime Network).
- Animation feel: 'exceptional sound design and animation work gives that perfect dodge such oomph that it hits the brain the same way a parry does in Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice' — combat described as 'matador matches... perform a chicuelina out of the way before closing in with the estocada' (Punished Backlog). Known flaw: 'The MC movement feels kinda off... the way she turns around is too rough, maybe adding some in-between animations could make it feel more fluid' (Steam community demo review).
- Structure: bosses only, no trash mobs — compared to Hyper Light Drifter (speed) and Shadow of the Colossus (boss-rush structure) by UK Anime Network. Demo = 2 bosses ('The Beast', 'The Guardian'), ~40 min, talisman build customization, post-clear leaderboard challenge mode. Community death stats: players 'died nearly six times their victories over the first two bosses combined.'
- Dev interview (UK Anime Network, first English interview): inspirations are Titan Souls (weak-point core), Sekiro + Dark Souls III (combat), Hollow Knight (silhouette — 'We aimed to create a similarly iconic and charming character'). Team roles: Wei-Sheng Liao (producer/lead programmer/combat design), Li-En Chen (animator/combat designer), Yi-Xuan Song (lead artist: character, environment, visuals), Pace Lin (assistant artist/programmer). ~3 years of development.
- Design process quote: 'We first create a complete battle using a blocky prototype character (which we affectionately refer to as the "tofu man"), and once the combat mechanics are set, we then design the boss's appearance.' Ruby was originally a hunter with 'a realistic rabbit head' — 'quite a terrifying design' — before becoming the cute hooded princess.
- UX friction reviewers flagged: boss intro cutscenes replay on every retry (skippable in ~2s but still criticized); demo bugs included looping gaps in cutscene music and interaction prompts failing to appear — polish of the retry loop was otherwise the most-praised trait.

## Actionable for Bloodgem

- Camera: use a 3/4 overhead view (Titan Souls-style), not straight vertical top-down — draw characters in three-quarter front/side view on a top-down ground plane. In Canvas this means y-sorted sprite rendering over a floor tilemap, with the world readable from above but faces/bodies readable from the side.
- Palette: reserve saturated blood-red/scarlet as the single signal color (weak points, focus marks, health, crits, key UI accents) against desaturated dark environments (near-black, cold greys, muted ambers). Rubinite's whole identity is 'ruby on darkness' — for Bloodgem, mirror Bloodborne's greys/blacks with crimson as the only loud hue.
- Sprite scale: small, chunky, highly readable player sprite (Titan Souls-scale, roughly 16-32px class) versus huge multi-tile bosses; render at integer pixel scale (imageSmoothingEnabled = false, fixed internal resolution upscaled). Fidelity contrast is the trick: simple gameplay sprites, lavish high-detail illustrated art for menus, portraits, and cutscenes.
- Lighting: implement dynamic 2D point lights with flicker (lanterns, torches, fire) composited over the pixel art — an offscreen light-map canvas with radial gradients and 'multiply'/'lighter' blending. Rubinite reviewers noticed its light sources specifically; also add drop shadows under every character to sell depth/verticality and to telegraph aerial boss attacks.
- Animation budget: concentrate it on the dodge moment — Rubinite's perfect dodge 'hits the brain the same way a parry does in Sekiro' through sound + animation + impact, not sprite fidelity. Give Bloodgem's dodge distinct audio, hitstop, a flash/particle burst, and an instant tangible reward (Rubinite grants instant Focus fill). And add turn in-between frames — the one animation criticism Rubinite got was rough direction changes.
- Combat structure to steal: a hold-button 'observe' mode that trades mobility for a proximity-scaled charge gauge (closer = faster) creates Bloodborne-style aggressive positioning in top-down form; stackable marks (max 3) that a hit can reset gives the risk/reward Bloodborne's rally system provides.
- Retry loop polish over content volume: Rubinite's 96%-positive demo is credited to instant restarts, consistent framerate, and intuitive controls — for Bloodgem, make death-to-retry under ~2 seconds and never replay cutscenes on retry (Rubinite was criticized for exactly this).
- Process: prototype bosses as untextured 'tofu man' blocks in Canvas first; only commit sprite art after the fight is fun. A 4-person team shipped Rubinite this way in ~3 years with one lead artist.
- UI style: minimal in-combat HUD (gauge + marks near the enemy, small health bar) contrasted with ornate, illustrated full-screen menus — keep the arena clean so the red signal color stays legible.
